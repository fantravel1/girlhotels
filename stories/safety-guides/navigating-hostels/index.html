The Solo Woman's Guide to Hostels

Everything you need to know about staying safe, making friends, and actually enjoying the hostel experience.

By Emma Rodriguez ‚Ä¢ Updated January 2025

I was terrified before my first hostel stay. Would my stuff get stolen? Would I feel unsafe? Would I be the "weird old person" (I was 28)? Five years and 100+ hostels later, I can tell you: with the right knowledge, hostels are incredible‚Äîand often safer than you'd expect.

This guide covers everything I wish I'd known before that first booking.

Choosing the Right Hostel

Not all hostels are created equal. Here's what to look for:

Read Reviews Strategically

  • Filter by "Solo Female": Hostelworld and Booking.com let you filter reviews by traveler type. This is gold.
  • Look for specifics: "Staff were helpful" is nice; "staff walked me to the metro at midnight" is better.
  • Ignore extremes: Both the one-star rants and five-star raves are often outliers. Focus on the 3-4 star reviews for honest assessments.
  • Recent reviews matter: Management changes everything. Prioritize reviews from the last 6 months.

Key Features to Look For

  • Female-only dorms: Not mandatory, but nice to have the option
  • Individual lockers: Big enough for your entire backpack
  • Keycard access to dorms: Not just the front door
  • 24-hour reception: Essential for late arrivals
  • In-room bathrooms: Avoid hallway showers if you can
  • Privacy curtains on bunks: Game-changer for sleep and security
  • Good common areas: This is where friendships happen

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Multiple reviews mentioning theft
  • "Party hostel" reputation (unless that's what you want)
  • No secure storage options
  • Poor lighting in common areas or hallways
  • Reviews mentioning staff being absent or unhelpful
  • Location in isolated areas with no public transport

Female-Only vs Mixed Dorms

There's no right answer‚Äîboth have their place.

Choose Female-Only When:

  • It's your first hostel experience
  • You value changing clothes in the room
  • The mixed-dorm reviews mention issues
  • You want a quieter environment
  • You're arriving very late at night

Mixed Dorms Are Fine When:

  • Reviews are universally positive
  • There are privacy curtains on beds
  • Bathrooms are in the room or right next door
  • The price difference is significant
  • You're traveling with male friends

Pro tip: Some hostels only have "female dorms" in name‚Äîcheck that they're actually enforced. Good hostels won't put a man in a female dorm even if it's the last bed.

Protecting Your Belongings

Theft in hostels is relatively rare, but it does happen. Here's how to minimize risk:

Essential Gear

  • Good padlock: Bring two‚Äîone for your locker, one for your bag. Combination locks are easier than keys you might lose.
  • Pacsafe or cable lock: For securing your bag to the bed frame
  • Money belt or hidden pouch: For passport and cards while sleeping
  • Small day bag with lock: For exploring while your big pack stays locked up

Locker Strategy

  • Put electronics, passport, and cards in the locker‚Äînot under your pillow
  • Use the locker every time you leave, even for "just a minute"
  • If lockers are too small, lock valuables inside your bag and cable-lock the bag to the bed
  • Keep a spare credit card and emergency cash separate from your main wallet

What NOT to Worry About

  • Clothes being stolen (virtually never happens)
  • Toiletries going missing (not worth worrying about)
  • Books or small items (use common sense, but don't stress)

Making Friends (Safely)

The social aspect is the best part of hostels. Here's how to meet people while staying safe:

Easy Conversation Starters

  • "Where are you from? How long are you traveling?"
  • "Any recommendations for [activity/food/neighborhood]?"
  • Join hostel activities‚Äîwalking tours, pub crawls, cooking nights
  • Sit in common areas with a book (but look approachable)
  • Ask if you can join a table at dinner

Safety Boundaries

  • Trust your instincts‚Äîif someone feels off, distance yourself
  • Don't share your room number with strangers
  • Meet new friends in public areas of the hostel first
  • Tell someone at reception if you're going out with people you just met
  • Share your location with a friend back home
  • Don't accept drinks you didn't watch being poured
  • It's okay to say "I'm tired" and go back to your room anytime

Hostel Etiquette

Being a good roommate keeps everyone happy and safe:

  • Plastic bags are your enemy: Pack in packing cubes that don't rustle at 6am
  • Prepare for morning: Set out clothes the night before
  • Headlamp vs phone flashlight: A red-light headlamp is way less disruptive
  • Quiet hours exist: Respect them‚Äîusually 10pm-8am
  • Don't have sex in dorms: This shouldn't need saying, but here we are
  • Be tidy: Keep your stuff contained to your bunk area

When Things Go Wrong

Know your options if you're uncomfortable:

  • Request a room change: Legitimate complaint? Most hostels will move you
  • Report creepy behavior: Staff want to know‚Äîit protects other guests too
  • Leave if you need to: Your safety matters more than a booking. Most platforms support refunds for safety issues.
  • Trust your gut: If something feels wrong, it probably is
