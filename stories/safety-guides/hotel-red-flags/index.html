Safety Guide

Hotel Red Flags

Warning signs to watch for when booking, checking in, and staying‚Äîand when to trust your gut and leave.

üìñ 10 min read üö® Safety Critical ‚ú® Updated 2025

The Most Important Rule: Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, it probably is. You can always find another place to stay. Your safety is worth more than any booking fee.

Red Flags in Reviews

Before you book, read reviews carefully‚Äîespecially recent ones from women traveling alone. Look for these warning signs:

üö© "Felt unsafe walking to/from the hotel"

Multiple reviews mentioning unsafe surroundings matter more than what the hotel itself looks like. Google Street View the area before booking.

üö© "Staff gave out my room number"

This is a serious security breach. Staff should never reveal which room you're staying in‚Äînot to "friends," not to anyone.

üö© "Someone tried to enter my room"

Whether it's "maintenance" without notice or unauthorized keycard access, this is unacceptable. One review like this is a red flag; multiple means avoid.

üö© "Locks didn't work properly"

Broken locks, doors that don't fully close, non-functional deadbolts. Basic security should always work.

üö© "Management blamed me for harassment"

If staff dismiss or minimize complaints from women, that hotel doesn't deserve your business.

Red Flags at Check-In

Your first impression matters. Pay attention to these signals when you arrive:

üö© They announce your room number loudly

Professional hotels write your room number down or say it quietly. If the desk clerk shouts "You're in room 302!" in a crowded lobby, ask to speak privately or request a different room.

üö© Ground floor room assignment (when you didn't request it)

Ground floor rooms have easier access‚Äînot ideal for solo women. Politely request a higher floor, away from stairwells and elevators.

üö© No front desk or security at night

Ask about 24-hour staffing. In budget hotels, this might mean reduced hours‚Äîknow what you're getting into.

üö© Unprofessional or overly personal comments

"Traveling alone? That's brave." Fine. "Traveling alone? No boyfriend?" Not fine. Trust how staff make you feel.

üö© The lobby feels sketchy

Loitering strangers, poor lighting, broken furniture, general neglect. If the public areas feel unsafe, the rooms won't be better.

Red Flags in Your Room

Do a quick safety check before settling in:

üö© Locks are damaged or don't engage properly

Test every lock: main door, deadbolt, chain/bar, connecting doors. If anything doesn't work, demand a new room or leave.

üö© Connecting door to adjacent room

Check that both sides are locked. If there's any doubt about security, ask for a room without a connecting door.

üö© Windows that don't secure or face accessible areas

Ground floor windows, windows facing fire escapes, or windows with broken locks are all concerns. Close curtains and check latches.

üö© No peephole or blocked peephole

You should be able to see who's at your door before opening it. If there's no peephole, use the door chain and only crack the door.

üö© No safe for valuables

Not a dealbreaker, but keep valuable items with you or use the front desk safe.

When to Leave Immediately

Some situations warrant an immediate exit, regardless of what you've paid:

  • Someone tries to enter your room without authorization
  • Staff refuse to help with a security concern
  • You're being followed or watched by other guests or staff
  • The lock on your door has been tampered with
  • You find evidence someone else has access to your room
  • The neighborhood becomes violent or unsafe
  • Your gut tells you something is very wrong

Document everything with photos and notes. Contact your credit card company for refund assistance. Report to the platform where you booked. Your safety matters more than money.

Pro Tips from Solo Women Travelers

üí° Use first name and initial only

Book as "J. Smith" rather than "Jennifer Smith." It doesn't reveal your gender or full identity.

üí° Put the "Do Not Disturb" sign on

Even when you're out. It signals the room is occupied. Order housekeeping when you need it rather than having someone enter while you're away.

üí° Keep a portable door alarm

Cheap, small, loud. Hangs on the door handle and screams if anyone opens it. Peace of mind for $10.

üí° Imply you're not alone

"My partner is meeting me later" or keeping men's shoes visible in the room. Not foolproof, but adds a layer.

üí° Share your location

Send your hotel details to someone at home. Use Find My Friends or share your Google Maps location. Check in regularly.

Green Flags to Look For

Women-only floors or wings, 24/7 staffing, key card elevator access, professional and discreet staff, strong reviews from solo women travelers, well-lit hallways and parking, and a genuine responsiveness to safety concerns.
