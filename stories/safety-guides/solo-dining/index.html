The Art of Solo Dining

How to eat alone, enjoy it, and discover that solo dining might become your favorite part of traveling.

By Alexandra Chen ‚Ä¢ Updated January 2025

I used to dread meals when traveling alone. I'd time restaurant visits to avoid peak hours, hide behind my phone, and eat as quickly as possible to escape the perceived judgment of other diners. I convinced myself that room service and takeaway were "easier."

Then something shifted. I started sitting at bars instead of tables. I put my phone away. I made eye contact with the bartender and asked for recommendations. I discovered that solo dining isn't something to endure‚Äîit's one of travel's greatest pleasures.

Here's everything I've learned about eating alone, confidently.

The Mindset Shift

First, let's address the elephant in the room: nobody is looking at you.

Seriously. That table of friends isn't pitying you. The couple isn't whispering about your single status. The waiter isn't judging you. Everyone is absorbed in their own experience. The discomfort is entirely internal‚Äîand that means you can change it.

Solo dining is increasingly common and normalized. In many cultures (Japan, France, Italy), eating alone is completely unremarkable. Once you release the fear of judgment, you'll discover something wonderful: eating alone lets you fully focus on the food, the atmosphere, and your own thoughts.

Reframe It

You're not eating alone because you have no one to eat with. You're eating alone because you chose to travel solo, which is brave and cool. You're treating yourself to a meal. You're having a date with yourself. You're living your life on your own terms.

Where to Sit

Seating choice makes a huge difference in how comfortable you'll feel. Here's the hierarchy:

ü•á The Bar

Hands down, the best spot for solo diners. You have automatic company (the bartender), entertainment (watching drinks being made, observing the room), and usually access to the full menu. Bar dining is so normalized that nobody will look twice.

ü•à Counter Seating

If the restaurant has counter seating facing the kitchen (like many Japanese restaurants or chef's tables), take it. You'll have a view, feel engaged, and often get extra attention from the chef.

ü•â Window or Terrace Table

If you must take a table, choose one with a view‚Äîeither of the street or the room. You'll have something to look at, and the position feels intentional rather than marooned in the middle of the room.

Avoid If Possible

Tables in the middle of the room, tables for four (where you'll feel the empty chairs), and corners where you're facing a wall.

The Phone Question

Here's my controversial opinion: put the phone away.

Yes, scrolling Instagram feels like a safety blanket. But it also signals "don't talk to me" and prevents you from actually experiencing the meal. You're in a foreign city eating amazing food‚Äîwhy would you stare at the same screen you look at at home?

Better Alternatives

  • Bring a book: More engaging than a phone and makes you look interesting
  • Bring a notebook: Journal, sketch, or plan tomorrow's adventures
  • People watch: One of travel's great pleasures
  • Talk to staff: Ask about the menu, the neighborhood, their recommendations
  • Just be: Sit with your thoughts. It gets easier with practice.

That said, if the phone helps you get through your first few solo meals, use it. The goal is enjoying yourself‚Äîhowever you get there.

Making Conversation

One of the underrated joys of solo dining is the conversations you'll have. Without a dining companion, you're available‚Äîto the bartender, the chef, the person sitting next to you.

Easy Openers

  • "What do you recommend?" (works every time)
  • "Is this your place?" (if they seem like an owner/chef)
  • "What's in this?" (about anything on the menu)
  • "I'm visiting from [place]‚Äîany local tips?"
  • Compliment something specific: the wine, a dish, the decor

You don't have to talk to anyone. But being open to conversation often leads to the most memorable travel moments‚Äîa recommendation that changes your trip, a story that moves you, a friendship that lasts.

üçï Casual Trattorias & Bistros

üç≥ Caf√©s & Brunch Spots

üç£ Omakase & Chef's Tables

Actually perfect solo‚Äîyou're interacting with the chef, and the experience is personal by design.

Fine Dining Alone

Yes, you can (and should) go to fancy restaurants alone. It's an act of self-love, and waitstaff at high-end restaurants are trained to make solo diners feel welcome.

Tips for fine dining solo:

  • Request bar seating when you book, if available
  • Go for lunch if dinner feels too intense‚Äîsame food, lighter atmosphere
  • Tell them it's special: "I'm treating myself" invites better service
  • Do the tasting menu: It's actually easier solo‚Äîyou just sit back and enjoy
  • Engage with the sommelier: They love helping someone who's genuinely curious

Practical Tips

  • Make a reservation: Some restaurants don't hold tables for one, so call ahead
  • Go early: First seating is often quieter and staff have more time for you
  • Sit where you can see: People-watching makes everything better
  • Order what you want: That appetizer that sounds good? Get two. Nobody's sharing.
  • Take your time: You're not waiting for anyone. Savor it.
  • Tip well: Staff remember solo diners who are kind and generous

When You're Just Not Feeling It

Some nights, you won't want to dine out alone. That's okay. Alternatives:

  • Food markets‚Äîwalk around, graze, sit on a bench
  • Hotel restaurants‚Äîfamiliar and easy
  • Takeaway in a park‚Äîa sunset picnic with yourself
  • Room service + a movie‚Äîsometimes self-care is staying in
  • Cooking classes‚Äîfood + social interaction

The goal isn't to force yourself to sit in restaurants every night. The goal is to not let fear of solo dining limit your choices.

The Rewards

Once you master solo dining, you unlock something precious: complete freedom. You can eat what you want, when you want, where you want. No compromising, no waiting, no "I don't know, what do you want?"

Some of my best travel memories happened at restaurant bars‚Äîconversations with chefs, recommendations that changed my trip, quiet moments of perfect food and complete contentment. You deserve those moments too.
