Your First Solo Trip: The Complete Guide

Everything you need to know to plan, book, and actually enjoy your first adventure traveling alone as a woman.

That was eight years and 40+ countries ago. Solo travel changed my life‚Äîand not in the clich√© way. It taught me I'm more capable than I thought, more adaptable, more resilient. It also taught me that fear is normal, preparation helps, and the first trip is always the hardest.

This guide is everything I wish someone had told me before that first flight.

Part 1: Choosing Your First Destination

Your first solo trip isn't the time to prove anything. Choose a destination that sets you up for success‚Äîsomewhere safe, easy to navigate, and forgiving for first-timers.

What Makes a Good First Solo Destination

  • High safety rating: Look for destinations with scores above 80 on our safety index
  • English widely spoken: Language barriers add stress‚Äîsave that for trip #3
  • Good tourist infrastructure: Easy transport, well-marked attractions, helpful locals
  • Walkable: Being able to explore on foot builds confidence
  • Solo traveler culture: Places where traveling alone is common and normalized
  • Reasonable flight time: Maybe not 18 hours for your first solo journey

Part 2: Booking Your Trip

Flights

  • Book arrival during daylight: Navigating a new city is easier when the sun is up
  • Avoid red-eyes if possible: You want to arrive alert, not exhausted
  • Screenshot your confirmation: Don't rely on wifi at the airport
  • Check visa requirements: Some countries need advance applications

Accommodation

For your first trip, prioritize safety and location over price. This isn't the time to save $20/night by staying somewhere sketchy.

What to Look For

  • 24-hour reception (crucial for late arrivals or emergencies)
  • Central location in a safe neighborhood
  • Reviews from solo female travelers
  • Good lighting and security measures
  • Walkable to public transport

Our recommendation: Book a well-reviewed hotel or upscale hostel for at least your first two nights. You can always change later, but arriving to a safe, reliable place reduces first-night anxiety enormously.

Trip Length

For a first solo trip, 5-7 days is ideal. Long enough to settle in and find your rhythm, short enough that homesickness won't hit hard. You can always book a longer trip next time once you know you love it.

Part 3: Pre-Trip Preparation

Essential Documents

  • Passport (valid for 6+ months after your return date)
  • Photos of all important documents stored in cloud + email
  • Travel insurance confirmation (don't skip this!)
  • Hotel reservation confirmations
  • Emergency contact list with local embassy info

Tell Someone Your Plans

Share your itinerary with a trusted friend or family member. Include flight details, hotel addresses, and a rough schedule. Set up location sharing on your phone. This isn't paranoid‚Äîit's smart.

Download Before You Go

  • Google Maps: Download offline maps for your destination
  • Google Translate: Download the language pack offline
  • Uber/local ride app: Set up before you arrive
  • WhatsApp: Essential for international communication
  • Your airline's app: For mobile boarding passes
  • Bank app: To monitor for fraud while traveling

Part 4: What to Pack for Safety

Security Essentials

  • Doorstop alarm: Wedges under the door and sounds if someone tries to enter ($10-15)
  • Padlocks: For hostel lockers or securing bags
  • Crossbody bag: Harder to snatch than a shoulder bag
  • Money belt: For passport and emergency cash
  • Portable charger: A dead phone is a safety risk

Clothing Tips

Research local dress norms. In some places, covering shoulders and knees helps you blend in and receive respectful treatment. Pack versatile pieces you can layer. Comfortable walking shoes are non-negotiable.

Part 5: Your First Day

The first day is often the hardest. You're jet-lagged, disoriented, and everything feels unfamiliar. Here's how to handle it:

  1. Get to your hotel safely: Pre-arranged transport or a reputable taxi/Uber
  2. Orient yourself: Walk around the immediate neighborhood in daylight
  3. Find essentials: Locate the nearest pharmacy, ATM, and grocery store
  4. Eat something: Jet lag is worse on an empty stomach
  5. Don't over-schedule: A simple walk and dinner is plenty for day one
  6. Get to bed at a reasonable local time: Even if you're not tired

Part 6: Dealing with Loneliness

Let's be honest: you will probably feel lonely at some point. It's normal. Here's what helps:

  • Schedule social activities: Food tours, walking tours, cooking classes‚Äîbuilt-in social time
  • Stay in hostels occasionally: Even private rooms put you near other travelers
  • Eat at the bar: Solo diners at restaurant bars often end up chatting with bartenders and neighbors
  • Join apps like Bumble BFF: Many solo travelers use this to meet other women
  • Call home: A video chat with friends can reset your mood
  • Remember it's temporary: Loneliness passes. The freedom remains.

Part 7: Staying Safe

Golden Rules for Solo Safety

  • Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, leave.
  • Don't tell strangers you're traveling alone or share your hotel details.
  • Stay aware of your surroundings‚Äîlimit phone use while walking.
  • Drink responsibly. Being impaired alone is risky.
  • Keep emergency numbers saved (local emergency + embassy).
  • Check in with someone back home regularly.

Part 8: Common First-Trip Fears (Addressed)

"What if I get lost?"

You have Google Maps. You have your hotel address written down. You can ask anyone. Getting a little lost is part of travel‚Äîand often leads to the best discoveries.

"What if something bad happens?"

This is why you have travel insurance, emergency contacts, and embassy information. Bad things can happen anywhere‚Äîincluding home. Preparation reduces risk.

"What if I'm lonely the whole time?"

You won't be. Travelers are friendly. Locals are curious. You'll have conversations. And solitude isn't the same as loneliness‚Äîyou might discover you love it.

"What if I hate it?"

Then you'll know‚Äîand you can go home. A bad trip is still an experience. But most people don't hate it. Most people wonder why they waited so long.

Final Thoughts

Your first solo trip will be imperfect. You'll make mistakes. You'll have awkward moments. You might cry in a bathroom somewhere (I did, in Barcelona, over a lost phone).

But you'll also have moments of pure magic‚Äîa sunset that takes your breath away, a conversation with a stranger that changes your perspective, a meal so good you close your eyes, a realization that you did this, alone, and you're okay.

The first trip is just the beginning. Book it. Go. You're more ready than you think.
