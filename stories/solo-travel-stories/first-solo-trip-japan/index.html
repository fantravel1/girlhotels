My First Solo Trip to Japan: A Complete Guide for Women

How I navigated Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka alone‚Äîand why Japan is the perfect first solo destination.

By Sarah Chen ‚Ä¢ 8 min read ‚Ä¢ January 2025

When I decided to take my first solo trip, everyone asked the same question: "Aren't you scared?" Japan was my answer to those fears. After two weeks exploring Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka completely alone, I can confidently say: if you're nervous about solo travel, Japan should be your first destination.

Why Japan Is Perfect for First-Time Solo Women

Japan consistently ranks as one of the safest countries in the world, and as a solo female traveler, I felt that safety every single day. I walked alone at 2 AM in Tokyo without a second thought. I left my phone at a restaurant and staff chased me down the street to return it. The cultural emphasis on respect and order creates an environment where you simply don't worry.

üõ°Ô∏è Safety Score: Japan's major cities score 90+ on our safety scale. Tokyo specifically scores 92/100 for women travelers.

Beyond safety, Japan is incredibly easy to navigate solo. Signs are in English, Google Maps works perfectly, trains run exactly on time, and konbini (convenience stores) are everywhere if you need anything at any hour.

Where I Stayed: Hotels That Welcomed Solo Women

Tokyo: The Hoxton Williamsburg... wait, no‚ÄîTrunk Hotel Shibuya

In Tokyo, I split my time between a capsule hotel (for the experience) and Trunk Hotel in Shibuya. The Trunk was perfect for solo travel‚Äîa gorgeous lobby where I could work during the day, excellent restaurant for solo dinners, and a location that made exploring easy.

For the capsule experience, I chose Nine Hours in Shinjuku, which has women-only floors. It was clean, futuristic, and surprisingly comfortable. I slept better than in many regular hotels.

Kyoto: A Traditional Ryokan

I splurged on two nights at a female-owned ryokan called Sakura House. Staying at a ryokan alone might seem intimidating, but the staff made me feel so welcomed. They explained every tradition, helped me with my yukata, and even packed me a bento for my train to Osaka.

üí° Pro tip: Many ryokans offer single-person rates that aren't much more than regular hotels. Don't skip this experience just because you're traveling alone!

Osaka: Generator Hostel

In Osaka, I wanted a more social atmosphere. The female-only dorm at Generator Osaka was great for meeting other travelers. I ended up exploring Dotonbori with two women I met at breakfast.

Practical Tips for Solo Women in Japan

Getting Around

  • Get a JR Pass before you arrive if traveling between cities
  • Download Google Maps offline for each city
  • IC Cards (Suica/Pasmo) work everywhere and make life easy
  • Women-only train cars exist during rush hour‚Äîlook for pink signs

Eating Alone

Solo dining is completely normal in Japan. Many restaurants have counter seating designed for solo diners. Ramen shops, sushi counters, and izakayas with counter bars are your best friends. I had some of my best meals at counters, chatting with the chef.

Communication

English is limited outside tourist areas, but Japanese people go out of their way to help. Download Google Translate for its camera function‚Äîpoint it at signs and menus for instant translation. Learn a few phrases: "sumimasen" (excuse me) and "arigatou gozaimasu" (thank you) go a long way.

The Moments I'll Never Forget

Watching the sunrise at Fushimi Inari shrine completely alone. The crowds don't arrive until 9 AM‚Äîat 5:30 AM, it was just me and the torii gates.

Getting lost in Golden Gai's tiny bars in Shinjuku. I was nervous to go alone, but the mama-san at one bar invited me to sit and practice my terrible Japanese while she taught me to fold origami.

Taking the wrong train in Osaka and ending up at a local festival by accident. Sometimes the mistakes are the best parts.

Would I Do It Again?

Absolutely. Japan gave me confidence as a solo traveler. The combination of safety, efficiency, and kindness made it the perfect training ground for independent travel. I've since traveled solo to Portugal, Thailand, and Mexico‚Äîbut Japan will always be where I discovered I could do this.

If you're considering your first solo trip, start here. Japan won't just be a vacation; it'll be a revelation of what you're capable of.
